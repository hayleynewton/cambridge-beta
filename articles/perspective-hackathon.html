Hackathon: Technology and Test Tasks

Introduction

Two teams had four days to each develop an idea around testing English language skills, working with a new technology. Both ideas used elements of GPS, one team chose augmented reality to test reading and listening, the other team GPS location points to test speaking and listening. Martin from the GPS team and Hayley from AR talk about their experiences and outcomes.

How did you approach your challenge?

Martin, GPS - We took a very lean approach, trying to iterate designs quickly, and talking the whole time. Keeping the conversation flowing helped identify and react quickly to little errors in the flow, and having other people available to validate ideas really allowed us to deliver something so polished in, basically, two days. The final day of the hackathon me and Kamil (UX) sat together and just designed and refined directly onto the screen as I built each page.

Hayley, AR - We approached the hackathon with an open mind, as with something so different and potentially complex, there is always a chance for the scope to change.
An initial flow was drawn down with a “mission” and we started from there.
We agreed that it would be important to show the following:

  • An example of augmented reality in real world, using specific locations
  • A start to finish user flow
  • An example of a potential listening element
  • An example of a potential reading element

What technology was used?

Martin - The base was Node.js, Google maps API, and HTML5 audio. From a front-end perspective, the magic happened with implementing an atomic design methodology. It’s a really interesting approach that lends itself to thinking of the smallest element of your UI, as a reusable component.

Hayley - This was the exciting bit for us!
There is a lot of tech we could use but to push ourselves out of our comfort zone we used the following:

  • Unity3D (games engine)
  • Vuforia (augmented reality library)
  • And C# Language

As we used a game engine, we had the opportunity to port to a number of devices including IOS and Android. This made device testing easy and allowed us to install it on our colleagues’ phones.

What did you learn from this hackathon?

Martin - The Atomic design approach (I’ve started using it across my personal projects), aside from being a seriously good way to organise CSS and components, gives you a common language when speaking with designers. It also tallies really nicely with the way a lot of the big front-end frameworks (react.js, vue.js) work, so you’ve also got a common language with the back-end dudes. On top of that, it’s actually made me re-evaluate (a bit) the most effective place for a front-end / UI dev to sit in a team (closer to UX & Design, than Dev – controversial, eh!)

Hayley - A valuable lesson we learnt when making an app that relied on GPS coordinates was its testing limitations… It takes time to walk around the office with phone in hand or take a walk outside every time you want to test!

From a technology perspective, it allowed to us explore beyond web work and try game and app development. Building the demo with a game engine was challenging and highly rewarding.

We also discovered that optimising an AR demo is essential as it was resource intensive.
Regular updates from the GPS, rendering of 3D objects and constant camera use drains battery and heats up your phone!
